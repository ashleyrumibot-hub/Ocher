TASTEFRAMEWORK · V1
BY BOZ ZOU · 2026-04-08

The Taste
Framework.

A rigorous, repeatable process for dissecting any visual corpus — a moodboard, a designer's body of work, a brand campaign — and translating it into a transferable creative system you can actually build from.

Most "inspiration" is passive. You scroll, you save, you forget. This framework is the opposite — it is the machinery for converting reference material into knowledge you can operate with. The output is not a gallery. It is a kit of parts, a list of principles, a set of refusals, and a series of drills. With that output you can go make work at the calibre of the reference.

Taste is not innate. It is a grammar — and grammars can be learned, but only by someone willing to parse the sentence, not just admire it.
[00] · GROUNDING
Theoretical shoulders.

This framework is not original theory. It is a synthesis of five established intellectual traditions, assembled into a practical pipeline. Each phase draws on at least one of them.

  • Erwin Panofsky — Iconological Method (1939). The three-layer model for interpreting images: pre-iconographic, iconographic, iconological. Originally for Renaissance painting. Applies cleanly to any visual artifact, including websites, packaging, and moodboards.
  • Roland Barthes — Semiotic Triad. Denotation, connotation, myth. Barthes extended this specifically to advertising, photography, and mass-media imagery in Mythologies and Image-Music-Text.
  • Pierre Bourdieu — Habitus & Taste. Taste is not innate; it is a socially-conditioned grammar. Decoding taste means decoding the class, subculture, and era it is speaking to.
  • K. Anders Ericsson — Deliberate Practice. Expertise comes from focused, feedback-driven repetition on specific weaknesses — not from volume of exposure. Internalizing taste requires drills, not scrolling.
  • Art Director Moodboard Practice. Every effective moodboard image answers a specific design question. The image is not inspiration; it is evidence for a claim.

The framework fuses these: formal analysis for evidence capture, semiotic/iconological layers for decoding, habitus thinking for audience modeling, and deliberate-practice drills for internalization.

[01] · OVERVIEW
Eight phases.

Phases one through four are analysis. Phases five through eight are synthesis. Skipping any phase collapses the output back into a moodboard.

The arrow of work runs left to right, but the loop closes: Phase 8 feeds back into Phase 1 with sharpened questions. Running the framework once on a corpus gives you an artifact. Running it three times on three corpuses gives you fluency.

[02] · PHASE ONE
Gather evidence.

Collect raw material with enough resolution to analyze, not just admire. Screenshots lie about rendering; computed CSS is ground truth. AI summaries hallucinate fonts; always verify from the live artifact.

Capture rules

  • Capture live state, not just screenshots. For a website, pull computed CSS (font-family, font-size, color, spacing) via devtools.
  • Capture verbatim text. Headlines, captions, nav labels, button copy, footer text. Voice is part of taste.
  • Capture the boring parts. Hero + body + footer. The "boring" parts reveal discipline.
  • Capture the negative space. What is not there? Refusals are data.
  • Capture context. Designer, client, date, industry. Taste only makes sense when situated.
  • Wide net before narrow. 15–30 references before filtering. Pattern recognition needs volume.

[03] · PHASE TWO
Atomize the visible.

Break every reference into elemental components so you can compare like-for-like across the corpus. This is Panofsky's pre-iconographic description: what is literally there, before interpretation.

For each reference, record the following twelve atoms. Be specific. "Minimalist" is not an atom; JetBrains Mono 12px 400 uppercase #000 on #FFF is.

The twelve atoms of visual taste

01 · Typography

Families, sizes, weights, casing, tracking, leading, and — most importantly — hierarchy device: how is hierarchy signaled? (Size? Position? Rule lines? Numbers?)

02 · Color

Shell palette vs content palette. Accent discipline. Pure vs warmed B&W. Where does color do emotional work vs structural work?

03 · Layout & Grid

Column count, asymmetry, weight, fixed vs fluid, rhythm. Does the design repeat a proportion often enough to become a signature?

04 · Space

Margin generosity. Vertical rhythm. Density (characters per viewport). Whitespace as element vs whitespace as gap.

05 · Imagery

Treatment (full-bleed / bordered / floating), aspect ratios, subject matter, color treatment, caption policy, density.

06 · Motion

Entrance reveals, easing, durations, hover behaviors, scroll behavior, heartbeat elements (clocks, cursors, status dots).

07 · Voice & Copy

Person, casing, adjective density, sentence length, verb vocabulary. Pull five or more verbatim quotes; they reveal diction.

08 · Iconography

Icons, decorative elements, logotype treatment, recurring motifs derived from the brand mark.

09 · Materials

Flat / skeuomorphic / glass / paper. Gradient discipline. Shadow discipline. Noise and grain.

10 · IA

Nav pattern, count of top-level destinations, homepage strategy, contact pattern, footer philosophy.

11 · Pacing & Narrative

Case-study structure. Image-to-text ratio. One argument per piece vs encyclopedic. Where are the cuts?

12 · Refusals

The negative-space inventory. What is conspicuously absent? The refusal list is often the most revealing data in the analysis.

Line up 15 references across these 12 atoms and the signature becomes visible. A single row (all references on one atom) often tells you more than a single column (all atoms on one reference).

[04] · PHASE THREE
Decode three layers.

Phase two was what is there. Phase three is what does it mean, why does it work, and whose taste is it speaking to. This is the heart of the framework — and the step most people skip.

Layer 1 — Denotation / Pre-Iconographic

The literal sign. "A black square in the top-left corner containing the letters STEVEN HANLEY in JetBrains Mono." This is just Phase 2 re-stated as descriptive sentences. Discipline yourself to describe before you judge.

Layer 2 — Connotation / Iconographic

The conventional meaning. What visual traditions does this belong to? What cultural codes is it speaking? (Brutalist web, Swiss modernism, Y2K, cyberpunk, quiet-luxury.) What other works does this echo? What tools, software, and subcultures does this aesthetic live in? What does the typeface connote, independent of the words? Trace the lineage.

Layer 3 — Myth / Iconological

The ideological work the design is doing. What worldview is it asserting as natural? Who is meant to feel recognized here? Whose taste is being validated — and whose excluded? What is the refusal list arguing? What story is the design telling about its maker? Every successful design naturalizes a worldview; making that worldview explicit is the heart of taste analysis.

If this design were a person, what would they refuse to wear? Answer that sentence and the iconological layer cracks open.
[05] · PHASE FOUR
Pattern the corpus.

Move from individual references to the corpus-level signature. Take your atomized evidence (Phase 2) and your decoded interpretations (Phase 3), then look across them.

Four searches

  1. Repetitions. What atoms recur across every reference? Same typeface? Same discipline? Same motif? Repetition across a corpus is the actual taste DNA.
  2. Refusals. What is consistently absent across every reference? Write a forbidden list. This list is often more definitive than the list of what's present.
  3. Tensions. What surprising contradictions hold the work together? A brutalist shell around lush content. Playful copy in a serious frame. Tensions are where work becomes memorable. Without tension, work is merely correct.
  4. Signature gestures. What one to three moves are unique to this maker? Gestures that would identify the work even if every label were stripped. Signatures are the fingerprint of taste.

The signature sentence

By the end of Phase 4 you should be able to write one sentence:

"The signature of this corpus is [X], achieved through [Y], defended by refusing [Z]."

If you cannot write that sentence, return to Phase 2. You have not looked long enough.

[06] · PHASE FIVE
Extract principles.

Convert the corpus-level signature into transferable rules you can apply to new work. Each principle must be written in a strict contract format: rule, why, when.

Principle contract

[RULE] — imperative, specific, testable
  [WHY]  — the reasoning; without it you cannot judge edge cases
  [WHEN] — the scope; when does this rule kick in, when not

Rules for writing principles

  • Aim for five to ten. Fewer than five means you haven't dug. More than ten means you haven't prioritized.
  • Each must be actionable. "Be restrained" is not a principle. "One font family, one size, one weight" is.
  • Pair every positive rule with a refusal. Positive rules say what to do; refusals say what never to do. Refusals are half of taste.
  • Test every principle against an edge case. If you can break your own rule in thirty seconds, it is not a rule.
  • Each principle must explain at least one specific artifact in the corpus. If it does not, it is speculation.

The refusal list

Parallel to your principles, keep an explicit list of the things the taste never does. A refusal list with 10–20 items is a sharper identity than any mission statement.

[07] · PHASE SIX
Build the kit.

Turn the principles into tokens and templates you can hand to yourself — or to a team — and get work at the reference calibre. The kit has five parts and lives in one document, not five.

01 · Tokens

Exact values, copyable into code or Figma. Type scale (families, sizes, weights, line heights, letter-spacing). Color palette with roles and hex. Spacing scale. Radius, shadow, border. Motion durations and easings.

02 · Components

The recurring structural pieces: metadata rail, case-study template, index/list component, hero treatment (or its explicit refusal), navigation pattern, footer pattern.

03 · Voice guide

Casing rules. Sentence-length targets. Forbidden adjectives. Verb vocabulary. Example sentences pulled verbatim from the corpus.

04 · Image direction brief

Subject-matter rules. Treatment rules (bleed, aspect, color, captions). A shot list, referenced from the corpus.

05 · Refusal list

Every element that is banned. Kept visible during work. When stuck, consult it before adding anything. The kit is only useful if it fits in one document — never five.

[08] · PHASE SEVEN
Drill with feedback.

Internalize the taste through focused repetition on specific weaknesses. Passive exposure does not build skill — Ericsson is unambiguous on this. You must practice the specific moves, get feedback, and iterate on the gaps.

Six drill types

  • Copy exercise. Redraw one reference from memory. Compare to the original. The gap between them is your drill target.
  • Constraint exercise. Build something in your style but forced through the reference's constraints. ("Build my site using only one font, one size, one weight, black on white.") Reveals your reflexes.
  • Refusal drill. Take a recent piece of your own work and apply the reference's refusal list to it. Cut everything the reference forbids. See if the piece gets stronger.
  • Translation drill. Take work from a totally different category and translate it into the reference's language. Tests whether you understood the principles or only the surface.
  • One-atom isolation. Pick a single atom (say, "hierarchy device") and design five versions of the same content that each use only that atom to create hierarchy. Masters one variable at a time.
  • Voice copy. Rewrite your most recent client brief in the reference's voice. Reveals your verbal reflexes and where they diverge.

Every drill needs a comparison point. Without comparison you cannot see what you are missing. Pair every drill with the original reference, a specific question, and a gap statement.

[09] · PHASE EIGHT
Critique and loop.

Ship work, compare to reference, log the delta, refine the kit. The framework is only useful if it closes into a loop.

The critique loop

  1. Produce a new piece using the kit.
  2. Set it beside two or three references from the original corpus.
  3. Walk the twelve atoms. On each atom, rate: matches / drifts / contradicts.
  4. Write the delta. "Mine is looser on spacing. Mine borrows color into the shell. Mine has captions where the reference would have none."
  5. Close one delta. Not all of them. The highest-leverage one.
  6. Iterate. Ship v2 with that delta closed.
  7. Log the lesson as a new principle or a refinement of an old one.
  8. Loop back to Phase 1 with sharpened questions.
Stop not when your work matches the reference — that is copying — but when you can produce work the original maker would recognize as kin without being able to tell you made it. That is internalization.
[10] · META
Rules above rules.

A handful of principles apply to the whole framework, not any single phase.

  • Never trust your first impression. The pre-iconographic phase exists because the brain skips straight to interpretation. Describe before you judge.
  • Never trust AI summaries of visuals. They hallucinate fonts, colors, and moods. Always verify from the live artifact.
  • The refusals are the soul. What the reference has is half; what it refuses is the other half. Both matter.
  • One sentence beats one paragraph. If the signature does not compress to one sentence, you have not finished.
  • Taste is situated. A design brilliant for recruiters is wrong for a community nonprofit. Always hold the audience in mind.
  • Taste compounds. Each corpus fully decoded sharpens the filter for the next. After five you will see taste the way a sommelier sees wine.
  • Ship the drill, not the essay. Every phase exists in service of Phase 8. The point is making work, not having a pretty document.

[12] · SCOPE
When to use — and not.

Use it when

  • Decoding a designer's body of work
  • Decoding a brand campaign (A24, Aesop, Off-White)
  • Decoding a moodboard before execution
  • Decoding a competitor before a pitch
  • Decoding your own past work to find your signature
  • Auditing a design system for hidden rules

Skip it when

  • Sketching for fun — don't overthink play
  • A single image with no corpus — not enough volume
  • Under an hour and you need to ship
  • You need raw novelty — the framework internalizes existing taste, it does not invent new ones

A framework is not a substitute for taste. It is the scaffolding that lets taste become portable — transferable from the person who made it to the person who wants to learn it, and then onward into new work.
